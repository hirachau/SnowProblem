Design

Introduction

Snow Problem is an action game where you twirl and flick snowflakes into your fiery enemies in the midst of a magical winter world. It's built for portability and uses the touchscreen exactly as you'd expect. Try your best to get a high score in time!

Description

In the main experience of this game, you'll find yourself plunged into the middle of a lonely and beautiful winterland - colored fire, snowflakes falling, and with soothing and slightly eerie background music to boot. Your objective? Use the touchscreen to swirl and flick the snowflakes into the correctly colored flames to weaken the threat to our peaceful winter...or something. Let's face it, you're actually doing it because it's fun.

You'll get points for matching up colors correctly and lose them for bad aim. It's simple enough, but surprisingly, is capable of drawing you in. You can control the trajectory of the snowflakes with simple flicks and drags of your finger, but be careful: it can be harder than it looks. You wouldn't want your score to go negative...

In addition, there are various other enhancements, features and modes planned for addition later on as development continues, to make the experience even more fun, so look forward to it. And development will continue for bug-fixes and improvements on a regular basis. Meanwhile, though, you'll have the core experience to keep you occupied.

Key Features

  • Simplicity and a clean, sharply contrasting look
  • Easy to pick up, hard to master
  • Use your fingers to blow snowflakes in arcs and spirals, sending them careening into the flames

Genre

This is a casual action game with an artistic and fantastical twist, meant to make your idle time feel less dreary.

Platform

Meant for a touch screen, this game is initially being developed for Android but has the potential to be ported to any touchscreen platform, potentially even Windows. There is currently no multiplayer support, but a future update may include online leaderboards.

Technical Analysis

The game consists mainly of three core components: the snowflakes, the enemies and the overall game controller itself.

The game controller determines the spawn locations of both enemies and snowflakes and generates the colours of the two. The more of a certain colour enemy on screen, the more likely a colour of snowflake will be generated to match it. The game controller also keeps track of the score and time, updating the UI accordingly. Once the time reaches nill, a call is made to load the high-score menu.

The Snowflakes are controlled by a fall script determining location prior to being interacted with by the user. The user interacts by putting their finger on the screen, generating a 'ray-cast' or straight line from the user's finger through the z axis to determine if any objects are hit along the way. Assuming an object is hit, an invisible spring is generated at the point of the user's touch and anchored to the object hit. Until the user releases their finger from the screen the spring will continue to drag the object to wherever the user points their finger. Upon release, the object is granted extra velocity in the z direction and unlocked to fly towards any fire.

The enemies are controlled by three different possible AI scripts at random on generation. One iteration has the enemy zig-zag, another has them stand still and the final one will simply make the enemy move back and forth in a straight line. From there it is a simple matter of whether the enemy detects a collision with the same colour snowflake, if it does both objects are deleted and points are increased. If a snowflake of incorrect colour is detected, the snowflake is deleted, and points are decreased.
